1. Executive summary

The macroeconomic environment of Thailand in the 2025–2026 operational cycle presents a structural complexity that challenges localized financial forecasting, retail consumption analysis, and equity valuation in the Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG) sector. Official state metrics — predominantly the headline Consumer Price Index (CPI) — broadcast a deflationary or near-zero inflation environment, finalizing 2025 with a full-year deflationary figure of −0.14%. However, this statistical deflation is heavily influenced by state subsidies on energy and structural weightings, which can diverge from the degradation of real purchasing power experienced by the Thai working and middle classes.

Retail consumers report acute inflationary pressure, a reality driven by aggressive corporate margin-protection strategies. To navigate rising input costs without triggering consumer backlash through nominal price hikes, FMCG manufacturers and retail conglomerates frequently deploy Price Pack Architecture (PPA). This manifests prominently as "shrinkflation" — stealthy reductions in product weight or volume. Because traditional CPI methodologies sample nominal shelf prices across a broad basket, they can structurally lag in capturing these stealth volume reductions in real-time, rendering the official inflation rate partially decoupled from street-level economic reality.

To address this macroeconomic blind spot, this research proposes the Thai Street Survival Index (TSSI) — a localized, shrinkflation-adjusted composite metric engineered to track the unadulterated cost of baseline survival in Thailand. Transitioning from theoretical proposal to a live pilot, the TSSI isolates the actual trajectory of localized purchasing power.

Pillar 1

Granular Price Per Unit (PPU) tracking

The TSSI tracks the exact price per gram and price per millilitre of its underlying assets. This mathematical conversion forces corporate shrinkflation to register immediately as an inflationary spike.

Pillar 2

Empirically weighted micro-basket

Five standardized, factory-sealed, non-substitutable Thai staples. Weights are derived from the NSO Household Socio-Economic Survey (COICOP 2018).

Pillar 3

Institutional-grade data architecture

Automated Python/Playwright scraping of Thailand's dominant e-commerce endpoints, smoothed with a 7-day rolling median to trim promotional noise.

2. Context and rationale: the macroeconomic disconnect

The situation: CPI weighting and supply-side dynamics

Entering 2026, Thailand's macroeconomic profile exhibits the hallmarks of a stagnant economy. Official data released by the Ministry of Commerce and the Bank of Thailand (BOT) indicates that the headline CPI spent much of 2025 in negative territory, ending the year at −0.14%. This trend deepened in early 2026, with CPI remaining constrained near the 100.27 point mark by March 2026 (a YoY inflation rate of −0.08%).

Energy and raw food constitute 30–40% of the national CPI basket; consequently, these two volatile categories dictate roughly 90% of the movement in Thailand's headline inflation. Since late 2025, the Thai government has implemented heavy subsidies on electricity bills and domestic fuel to cap energy costs, while favourable weather temporarily suppressed raw food prices. When these highly weighted, state-managed categories are stripped away, core inflation remained positive, accelerating slightly to +0.60% year-over-year in early 2026.

The complication: Price-Pack Architecture and shrinkflation

While energy costs are subsidized, FMCG corporations face the unyielding realities of rising global supply chain costs. To protect profit margins, these entities utilize Price Pack Architecture — the systematic optimization of a product portfolio via manipulation of price points, packaging configurations, and product attributes.

When manufacturers understand that the consumer is highly price-elastic, they deploy PPA to execute hidden price increases — primarily shrinkflation (volume downsizing), maintaining the nominal shelf price while covertly reducing net weight or volume. In Thailand, this phenomenon is rampant; an Ipsos survey found that 39% of Thai consumers actively noticed reductions in product sizes while prices remained static.

The resolution: a PPU-adjusted micro-basket

To definitively bypass the smoothing effect of broad-basket subsidies and corporate Price Pack Architecture, macroeconomic analysts require a supplementary framework. The resolution is the TSSI — a targeted micro-basket comprising factory-sealed, culturally ubiquitous goods, tracked exclusively by their price per unit. By narrowing focus to highly inelastic assets and using automated extraction to track the exact cost per gram and millilitre, the TSSI creates a mathematical baseline of survival costs that reflects the reality of purchasing power.

3. Methodology and mechanics: the architecture of the TSSI

3.1 Basket asset definition, selection, and weighting

The TSSI basket contains five standardized items mapped to the Thai NSO's Classification of Individual Consumption by Purpose (COICOP 2018). See Appendix A.1 for the full derivation matrix.

  • Asset A — Raw carbohydrate (Mahboonkrong Jasmine Rice 5 kg): 25%. The absolute fundamental staple of the Thai diet; a vital anchor for un-subsidized, raw caloric cost.
  • Asset B — Processed carbohydrate (Mama Tom Yum Koong pack): 15%. Extreme nominal-price rigidity (6 baht pegged 14 years before moving to 7 baht) makes the pack a highly sensitive gauge of underlying raw-material inflation.
  • Asset C — Prepared protein (7-Eleven EZYGO Kaphrao chilled box): 35%. CP All's 14k-store retail footprint makes EZYGO pricing a de facto national standard, stripping vendor portion variability.
  • Asset D — Essential hydration (Crystal Drinking Water 600 ml): 15%. A standardized, factory-sealed 600 ml PET bottle captures fundamental liquid-intake costs without luxury markups.
  • Asset E — Labour stimulant (M-150 150 ml glass bottle): 10%. A pristine proxy for the fundamental cost of sustaining blue-collar Thai labour.

3.2 Data intake architecture and legal compliance

The TSSI employs a fully automated programmatic web-scraping pipeline targeting the primary digital endpoints of Thai retail (7-Eleven delivery API, Shopee, Lazada). The extraction layer is built on Playwright — a headless browser automation framework capable of fully rendering dynamic DOM elements and JavaScript-heavy e-commerce architectures.

Legal and ethical compliance. The extraction protocol strictly targets publicly available retail pricing data. It collects no Personally Identifiable Information, in full compliance with Thailand's Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) B.E. 2562. Polite request rates and robots.txt are honoured; no authorization mechanisms are bypassed.

Imputation and fallbacks. Recognizing the inherent fragility of web scraping, the database employs a forward-fill (ffill) methodology: if a scrape fails or an item is temporarily out of stock, the previous day's PPU is carried forward. To protect data integrity, any asset experiencing a scrape failure for more than 3 consecutive days is flagged as structurally missing rather than imputed, and the composite weight is renormalized across remaining assets.

3.3 Composite score calculation and noise smoothing

The quantitative engine calculates the Price Per Unit (PPU) for each asset as a database GENERATED ALWAYS column: 

PPU = nominal_price / net_weight (or net_volume)

The composite index applies the NSO-derived weights to each asset's relative PPU, anchored to a normalized baseline of 100 on {{ baseline_date }}: 

TSSI(t) = ( Σᵢ  Wᵢ · PPUᵢ(t) / PPUᵢ(0) ) × 100

Addressing data volatility. E-commerce platforms use algorithmic dynamic pricing and short-lived promotions that diverge from static physical retail shelf prices. The TSSI applies a 7-day rolling median filter to per-asset PPU before composite calculation, trimming outlier promotional discounts and isolating the structural trajectory.

4. Methodological limitations

  1. The skimpflation paradox

    The TSSI scores exceptionally high on shrinkflation resistance (anchored to physical weight) but cannot detect skimpflation — the degradation of ingredient quality. If a manufacturer holds a 90 g pack weight constant but substitutes premium oils for cheaper palm oil, the PPU registers zero change. The TSSI therefore has a downward directional bias, likely understating true inflation.

  2. E-commerce vs physical divergence

    Despite rolling-median smoothing, relying on digital delivery endpoints means the index maintains a slight bias toward e-commerce pricing dynamics, which may occasionally decouple from provincial, cash-based physical retail.

  3. Pipeline fragility

    Web scraping commercial platforms is inherently brittle. Unannounced DOM restructuring requires continuous manual maintenance of the Playwright extraction scripts.

Divergence testing (planned, not yet applicable)

Divergence testing against headline CPI is a planned deliverable, not a current finding. The intended procedure interpolates monthly official CPI to daily via cubic spline, aligns it with the smoothed TSSI, then runs an Engle–Granger cointegration test on the two series; failure to reject the null of no cointegration (p > 0.05) would be evidence the TSSI and headline CPI have structurally decoupled. This test is deliberately not reported on the current series because a CPI‑derived index cannot be validly tested for cointegration with CPI itself — the result would be spurious by construction. The test becomes meaningful once the index is powered by Tier 1 / Tier 2 data (see roadmap below).

Appendix C · Data roadmap (how this pilot graduates to production)

The current pilot ships a complete framework powered by a CPI‑derived backbone. The roadmap below is the explicit path for retiring that backbone in favour of fully independent observations. Each tier is additive: moving to Tier 2 does not require abandoning Tier 1.

  1. Tier 1 · Official Thai government price data (spine)

    Replace the CPI‑interpolation layer with direct retail price series from the Bank of Thailand Statistical Data Warehouse and the Department of Internal Trade (DIT) weekly bulletins. Both are free, public, legally scrapeable, and cover rice, instant noodles, and bottled water directly. Expected impact: CPI‑derived share drops from ~88% to <10% of observations, replaced by first‑party government retail surveys. New adapter: app/backfill/bot_sdw.py, app/backfill/dit_bulletin.py.

  2. Tier 2 · Manual weekly panel (shrinkflation signal)

    One human, one reference 7‑Eleven, five SKUs, one photo each, one weekly CSV commit. This is the only tier that actually captures shrinkflation — net weight off the package, price off the shelf, photo audit trail in data/receipts/. Optionally opened to public contributors via a /submit form with admin review before rows land in data/observations.csv.

  3. Tier 3 · Opportunistic e‑commerce scraping (cross‑check)

    The existing Playwright pipeline is retained but reframed as a second opinion, not the primary feed. Replace placeholder URLs in app/scraper/assets.py with canonical product pages; cap Tier 3's weight at ≤20% of any day's composite so a DOM break or bot‑challenge on Lazada / Shopee / 7‑Eleven Delivery never invalidates the index. Honoured robots.txt, polite rates, PDPA compliant.

  4. Tier 4 · Historical backfill via Wayback (government bulletins)

    Point the existing Wayback CDX adapter at dit.go.th weekly bulletin PDFs, not at e‑commerce product pages. Archive coverage of government bulletins is dense and URL‑stable, yielding a real multi‑year backbone with ~50 lines of scraping code.

  5. Divergence testing

    Once Tiers 1–2 dominate the observation set, the Pearson + Engle–Granger cointegration test described in A.2 becomes statistically meaningful and will be reported here with the test statistic and p‑value.

The full framework — five‑asset COICOP‑mapped basket, PPU calculation, 3‑day ffill cap with structural‑missing flagging, 7‑day rolling median smoothing, weight renormalization, and Appendix B API contract — is source‑agnostic. Swapping source tiers is a data‑layer change only; nothing in the calc engine or API surface needs to move.
